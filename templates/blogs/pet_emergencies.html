Golden Rules of Pet Emergency Response

Essential First Steps in ANY Emergency

1Stay Calm: Your pet can sense your anxiety. Take deep breaths and think clearly.

2Ensure Safety: Remove your pet from immediate danger without putting yourself at risk.

3Assess Quickly: Check for consciousness, breathing, and obvious injuries.

4Call for Help: Contact your veterinarian or emergency clinic immediately.

5Transport Safely: Move injured pets carefully to avoid further harm.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL WARNING

Injured animals may bite or scratch out of fear and pain, even if they're usually gentle. Approach cautiously and consider muzzling if necessary (but never muzzle if vomiting or having breathing difficulties).

Life-Threatening Emergencies: Act NOW

ü´Å Choking Emergency

Immediate Actions for Choking

1Open the mouth: Look for visible foreign objects. DO NOT use your fingers if you can't see the object clearly.

2For Small Dogs (under 30 lbs):

  • Hold upside down by hind legs
  • Give 5 sharp taps between shoulder blades
  • Check mouth for dislodged object

3For Large Dogs:

  • Lift hind legs (wheelbarrow position)
  • Push firmly upward on belly just below rib cage
  • Alternative: Lay dog on side and push firmly on belly

4For Cats: Hold by hind legs, head down, and tap sharply between shoulder blades.

üéØ Prevention Tip

Common choking hazards: small balls, bones, rawhide pieces, toys smaller than your pet's throat. Always supervise playtime and choose appropriately sized toys.

ü©∏ Severe Bleeding

Bleeding Control Steps

1Apply Direct Pressure: Use clean cloth, gauze, or towel. Press firmly over the wound.

2Do NOT Remove Saturated Bandages: Add more layers on top; removing bandages disrupts clotting.

3Elevate if Possible: Raise injured limb above heart level if no fracture is suspected.

4Pressure Points: For leg bleeding, apply pressure to pressure points on inner thigh (rear legs) or armpit area (front legs).

5Transport Immediately: Continue pressure during transport to veterinary clinic.

üíñ Not Breathing/No Pulse

CPR for Pets (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

1Check Airway: Open mouth, extend neck, check for obstructions.

2Rescue Breathing:

  • Close pet's mouth, breathe into nose
  • Watch for chest rise
  • Give 1 breath every 3 seconds

3Chest Compressions:

  • Small dogs/cats: One hand on each side of chest, compress 1/2 to 1 inch
  • Large dogs: Lay on side, compress over widest part of chest 1-3 inches
  • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute
  • Ratio: 30 compressions to 2 breaths

Common Emergency Situations

ü§¢ Poisoning

‚ò†Ô∏è TOXIC SUBSTANCES - NEVER INDUCE VOMITING FOR:

  • Bleach, drain cleaners, toilet bowl cleaners
  • Gasoline, kerosene, motor oil
  • Sharp objects (bones, glass)
  • Unknown substances

Poisoning Response Protocol

1Identify the Poison: Keep packaging/container for vet reference.

2Call Poison Control: Contact vet immediately for guidance on whether to induce vomiting.

3If Vomiting is Advised: Use 3% hydrogen peroxide (1 teaspoon per 5 lbs of body weight, maximum 3 tablespoons).

4Do NOT induce vomiting if your pet is unconscious, having seizures, or ingested corrosive chemicals.

5Seek Veterinary Help Immediately: Poisoning is time-sensitive; every minute counts.

üí° Prevention Tip

Keep all medications, cleaning products, and toxic foods (like chocolate, onions, grapes, xylitol) securely stored away from pets.

üå°Ô∏è Heatstroke

Signs & Emergency Actions

  • Excessive panting, drooling, collapse, red gums
  • Move pet to a cool area immediately
  • Apply cool (not ice-cold) water to body
  • Place wet towels around neck, under armpits, and between legs
  • Offer small amounts of cool water to drink
  • Transport to vet urgently

‚ö° Seizures

What to Do During a Seizure

1Keep pet away from stairs, furniture, and sharp objects.

2Do NOT put anything in the mouth ‚Äì they cannot swallow their tongue.

3Time the seizure (most last 1‚Äì3 minutes).

4After seizure, keep pet calm and warm until fully conscious.

5Call your vet if seizure lasts >5 minutes or repeats within 24 hours.

ü¶¥ Broken Bones / Fractures

Steps to Stabilize

1Do NOT try to reset the bone.

2Keep pet as still as possible.

3Gently place a rigid support (like a magazine or stick) beside the injured limb and loosely bandage to stabilize.

4Transport to the vet immediately.

Pet First Aid Kit Essentials

  • Sterile gauze, bandages, adhesive tape
  • Antiseptic wipes, pet-safe wound cleaner
  • Hydrogen peroxide (3%)
  • Digital thermometer
  • Tweezers and blunt scissors
  • Muzzle or soft cloth (for restraint)
  • Instant cold packs
  • Emergency contact numbers

üéí Pro Tip

Keep a smaller first aid kit in your car for outdoor adventures ‚Äì emergencies often happen away from home.

Conclusion

Being prepared for pet emergencies can save your pet's life. While first aid is never a substitute for veterinary care, knowing how to act quickly will stabilize your pet until professional help is available. Print this guide, keep it with your first aid kit, and review it regularly to stay confident in case of crisis.
